the year was 1995.

Heads turned as Gary Kremen stepped into his office, dressed in a rainbow tie-dyed tee. The founder of online dating site Match.com, still in its infancy at the time, had arrived for his first television interview, conducted while perched comfortably in a beanbag chair. It was during this infamous Q&A that the CEO would unwittingly predict the future. He looked into the camera and, making eye contact with the audience watching at home, uttered words more truth than he ever could have realized:

"Match.com will bring more love to the planet than anything since Jesus Christ."

Nearby, Match.com communications director was stunned; surely, she thought, this would be the moment to end the company for good.
But as time went on, she, along with the the rest of the world, came to realize...

Kremen was right.

Over the course of the next 20 years, what began as a last-resort for the lonely cat women of the 90s would transform into one of the most innovative, exciting, convenient, frustrating, reinvigorating, mind-boggling tools for 21st-century singles:

online dating.

...And that was only the beginning.

As online dating became destigmatized and the number of users grew, a variety of cohorts joined Match.com in the world of data-driven dating. One of the most famous, eHarmony, arrived in August 2000, founded by clinical psychologist Dr. Neil Clark Warren and his son-in law. Known for its lengthy application process, including a questionnaire that once included 450 questions, it became the first algorithm-based dating site. At its height, the service boasted 33 million users. In 2015, it was reported that 71% of women and 69% of men met their spouse within a year of creating an eHarmony account.

The mid-to-late-2000s and the introduction of the smartphone offered another platform on which internet dating could thrive. Within the next decade, the number of online dating services available to hopeful singles skyrocketed as mobile-first apps overtook traditional websites. By 2013, an estimated 8,000 online dating services were available worldwide, 2,500 of which existed in the U.S.

Today, over 95 million people date online.

That's a lotta love.

In 1995, when Match.com first launched, only 25 million people worldwide had internet access. In 2015, that number reached 3.578 billionâ€”and of those, 95 million are seeking relationships online. That same year, it was estimated that one in five new relationships and one in six marriages begin online.

In the United States alone, millions of people pursue love via the internet. As of December 2017, nearly 30 million hopeful singles are searching for significant others on six primary sites: Tinder, PlentyOfFish, Match.com, OKCupid, Grindr and Bumble.

why?

People using online dating services tend to log on for a variety of reasons, and sometimes more than one reason at once. According to data from April 2017, the majority of surveyed users reported using online dating for three reasons: to meet people who shared their interests, to meet people who shared their values or to find someone for a long-term relationship or marriage. Honorable mention: those searching for "fun" came in at 26%.

Check out the United States' six most popular dating apps*.

*as of 12/2017

#1 Tinder

Launched in 2012 by Hatch Labs, Tinder caught fire almost overnight; within a few months of its initial release, the app rose to become one of the 25 most popular social networking apps available online. The app allows users to swipe right (like) or swipe left (dislike) on other users profiles, and allows two users to chat if both swipe right on one another. Currently the most popular dating service on the market, the app generates about one billion swipes per day.

Sign up for Tinder

#2 PlentyOfFish

PlentyOfFish originally launched as a website in 2003. The app tends to cater to slightly older users, as well as those looking for long-term relationships rather than casual meet-ups or short-term connections. Although popular primarily in markets like Canada and the United Kingdom, the app still ranks as one of the United States' top dating apps with 6.7 million American users as of December 2017.

Sign up for PlentyOfFish

#3 Match.com

Launched in 1995 by Gary Kremen and Peng T. Ong, Match.com is often considered the grandfather of all dating apps. It was the first to utilize the traditional questionnaire method in which users respond to a variety of questions and receive potential matches based on the compatibility of their answers with those of other users. A giant in the online dating world, the Match.com parent company also owns Tinder, OkCupid and PlentyOfFish. As of 2017, the app boasts 5.1 million users in the United States.

Sign up for Match.com

#4 OkCupid

Founded by the same team that brought the internet SparkNotes, OkCupid launched in January 2004. The website takes a more traditional approach to online dating services, requiring users to create a profile and fill out an initial questionnaire focusing on their most basic desired traits in a partner, including age, location, gender identity, sexual identity, political identification, and the like. Users later have the option to fill out additional questions on a variety of topics, ranging from lifestyle choices to questions like "What's better: Kissing in Paris, or kissing in a tent in the woods?" As of December 2017, OkCupid boasts 5.1 million users in the U.S.

Sign up for OkCupid

#5 Grindr

Launched in March 2009, Grindr is both the largest and most popular gay mobile app community in the world. Aimed at helping gay and bisexual men meet, the interface arranges users in order by location, with those closest showing at the top of the grid. The app allows users to chat and send photos, as well as share precise locations. The app has won a number of "Best Dating App" awards and has been celebrated for its support of the LGBT community, especially in matters regarding safe sex and equality. As of 2017, the app has 27 million users worldwide.

Sign up for Grindr

#6 Bumble

Released in December 2011, Bumble aims to foster romantic, platonic and professional relationships. Users have the option to swipe left (dislike) or swipe right (like) on other users' profiles. In heterosexual matches, only the female user can initiate contact; Bumble also offers "BFF Mode" and "Bumble Bizz" for making friends and networking, respectively. In all modes, matches last just 24 hours before "expiring," encouraging users to swipe right only on those they are certain they would like to speak to right away. As of November 2017, the app had 22 million users, almost half of whom are female.
